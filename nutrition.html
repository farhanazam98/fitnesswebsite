When it comes to fitness, nutrition is as important, if not more important, than exercise itself. Nutrition plays an important in whatever your fitness goals are, whether it is gaining muscle, losing fat, or maintaining your current state.

Macronutrients

When it comes to macronutrients, the three categories are as follows:

  • Carbs
  • Fats
  • Protein

Each of these plays a different role in the body, and every food that you eat contains at least one of these macronutrients. I will break down each of these macronutrients in the following section.

Carbohydrates

Carbs are usually the body's main fuel source. Carbohyrdates are broken down into either glucose to be used immediately as energy, or as sugar which is stored to be used later as a source of energy. Carbs often get a bad rep as the cause for fat. However, the right amount of carbs from healthy sources is important to the function of the body. Also, athletes tend to need more carbs to provide additional energy for peak performance.

An important note is that not all carbs are created equally. Although it is a bit of an oversimplification, we can divide carbs between naturally occuring carbs vs. processed carbs. Naturally occuring carbs (e.g. whole grain bread, fruits, vegetables, beans) are high in fiber, vitamins, and nutrients. This helps us feel satiated. On the other hand processed carbs, (e.g. white bread, white rice, white flour) lack fiber, vitamins and minerals. This is not optimal for health or fitness goals.

Protein

Protein is essential for both building and maintaining muscle. Protein gets a bad rap sometimes. Some people believe that eating too much protein can lead to a "bulky" look, while others attest that too much protein is bad for the kidneys. Both of these statements are false (citation needed). Protein is a satiating nutrient, meaning it helps us feel full while we are eating. Also, adequate protein helps develop that "toned" look that many people strive for. I would argue that animal products are the best way to consume adequate amounts of protein, there are definitely other ways to supplement protein, either through whey tofu, beans, eggs, etc.

Once again, not all protein is created equal. Consuming lots of processed meats is not generally recommended. Some proteins are leaner (contain less fat) than others. For example, tuna, chicken breast, and egg whites contain little to no fat, while steak, chicken thigh/leg and pork contain higher amounts of fat.

One last note is that protein is somewhat of a costly macronutrient to come across. A lot of restaurants offer dishes higher in carbs/fat rather than protein. Buying ready-to-eat meats are generally more expensive. These costs can be offset by buying in bulk and cooking these meats at home

Fats

Fats are an essential component of one's nutrition as well. Fats play a key role in regulating the body's hormones, including leptin, cortisol, and growth hormone. These hormones play a vital role in regulating how hungry we feel, our stress, energy, and fat metabolism. Not eating enough fats can have a detrimental effect one one's health and fitness goals. Fat is also the most satiating macronutrient. Eating enough (healthy) fats could help curb one's appetite. When the body runs low on carbs, the body turns to fat as a source of energy. This is the basis for the ketogenic diet, which has worked positively for a lot of people.

Once again, not all fats are created equal. Fats can be generalized into three broad categories:

  • Trans
  • Saturated
  • Unsaturated

Trans fats are not a necessary component of one's diet and should be avoided at all costs. These fats are associated with an increased risk of coronary artery disease, amongst many other health conditions. Luckily, the government has implemented laws to limit the amount of trans fats in restaurants.

Saturated fats are an important component in one's nutrition, but only in limited amounts. It is generally recommended that 5-6% of daily caloric intake should come from saturated fat. Amounts larger than are not recommended and lead to cardiovascular disease(citation).

Unsaturated fats (aka polyunsaturated fat, and monounsaturated fat) are generally accepted as the healthiest form of fat. This comes from foods such as plant-based food sources, like vegetable oil, nuts. They can also be found in fish. Polyunsaturated fats contain Omega 3 fatty acids, which are for heart health.
